# Veil of Ignorance

## The Forcing Function

John Rawls's thought experiment (*A Theory of Justice*, 1971): design the rules of
society from behind a "veil of ignorance" — without knowing which position you'll occupy.
Would you design it differently if you might be born poor, disabled, a racial minority,
an immigrant, a worker rather than a shareholder? If yes, you weren't designing for
everyone. You were designing for your actual position and calling it universal.

The operational version isn't a theory of justice. It's a forcing function for
stakeholder enumeration:

> **If you didn't know which position you'd occupy — if you could be anyone this
> affects — what would you want this analysis to account for?**

Most analysts unconsciously optimize for the position they actually occupy. The veil
forces enumeration of stakeholders who'd otherwise stay implicit.

## Investment Analysis: The Multi-Stakeholder Frame

Standard investment analysis is built for one stakeholder: the shareholder. Workers,
communities, ecosystems, and future generations appear as inputs (labor costs), risks
(regulatory exposure), or externalities (someone else's problem). The veil interrupts this.

**Before publishing an investment thesis, ask:**

- If you were a worker at this company rather than a potential shareholder, what would
  you want this analysis to surface? (Wage suppression as margin expansion looks different
  from that position.)
- If you were a member of the community where the company operates, what would you want
  this analysis to notice? (Externalized costs — pollution, housing pressure, water use —
  that don't show up in earnings but do show up in someone's life.)
- If you were a future generation inheriting the consequences of this company's current
  operations, what would you want the analysis to flag?
- If you were one of the excluded companies — the ones this firm's ethical screen removes —
  what argument would you make that this analysis is applying principles inconsistently?

The last question is the sharpest. Inconsistent application of ethical screens is a
standing problem: the screen reflects which harms the analyst can see from their own
position. The veil forces the question: what harms are invisible to me because I benefit
from their invisibility?

## Exclusions Research: The Asymmetry Test

For ethical exclusion decisions specifically: the veil reveals a systematic asymmetry.
Harms that fall on people the analyst resembles are more legible than harms that don't.

Ask:
- Is this exclusion criterion applied with equal force across industries that produce
  equivalent harm but different profiles of victims?
- Are the harms this screen is designed to prevent harms the analyst has ever been
  exposed to? If not, is the analyst relying entirely on second-hand definitions of
  the harm?
- Would the companies excluded by this screen recognize the picture being drawn of
  them? If they'd object that the harm is mischaracterized, what's their argument?
  (They might be wrong. You should still know what it is.)

## Policy and Product: When to Apply

- **Policy writing**: Who benefits? Who bears the cost? If your answer would change
  depending on your own position, you have a distribution problem.
- **Product decisions**: If you were using this as a first-generation immigrant, as
  someone on a slow connection, as someone whose legal name doesn't match their
  preferred name — would it work?
- **Research framing**: Whose questions are being asked? Who defines what counts as
  a problem worth studying?

## The Hard Version

The easy version stops when you've named the obvious populations: workers, poor people,
racial minorities, disabled people. These have enough institutional recognition to
appear on the standard checklist.

The hard version asks: **what populations aren't on the checklist yet because they
don't have enough institutional recognition to be on it?**

**If the question feels answerable, you stopped looking too soon.**

The veil is most useful at the edge of what you can see. One hundred years from now,
which stakeholders this analysis ignores will seem obviously relevant? You probably
can't answer that from inside your own position. But asking it, and sitting in the
discomfort of not being able to answer it, is different from not asking.

## What the Veil Doesn't Do

The veil generates questions. It cannot answer them.

It can tell you that a decision might be experienced very differently depending on
position. It cannot tell you what that experience actually is. For that, you need
sources with standing — people who've lived it.

**The veil generates the question. Standing answers it.**

Using the veil to feel like you've considered everyone is a bypass, not a method.
The output of this exercise should be a list of questions you don't yet have answers
to — not a confirmation that you've covered the bases.

## The Rawls Critique

The veil assumes a liberal subject who can coherently imagine themselves as "anyone."
Intersectionality research (Crenshaw, Collins) complicates this: you can't just imagine
yourself as a different demographic variable. The intersection produces dynamics that
can't be derived from the parts.

The veil gets you partway. It interrupts the default of optimizing for your actual
position. It doesn't substitute for standpoint knowledge, and imagining yourself as
"anyone" is not the same as actually occupying those positions.

## Connection to Anti-Racist Review

The veil appears in the `anti-racist-review` skill as one of eight critical questions,
applied specifically to writing about marginalized communities.

This standalone skill is for the broader case: any analysis where the analyst's position
shields them from some of the consequences — investment research, policy, product,
institutional design.

The "if the question feels answerable, you stopped looking too soon" heuristic applies
in both contexts. The difference is scope: anti-racist-review asks it about specific
communities; this skill asks it about any affected stakeholders.
