# Intellectual Tradition

The questions in the SKILL.md didn't come from nowhere. These are the thinkers whose
work runs underneath them. This file exists so the skill accumulates rather than
starting from scratch each time.

---

## Miranda Fricker — *Epistemic Injustice* (2007)

Two categories that do real work:

**Testimonial injustice**: A credibility deficit is given to a speaker because of who
they are — identity as a discount applied to what they say before the content is even
evaluated. The inverse is testimonial privilege: credibility excess given to the
dominant identity. Both operate without anyone deciding to do them. The wronged party
often has no name for what happened.

**Hermeneutical injustice**: A gap in the collective interpretive resources leaves
someone unable to make intelligible sense of their own experience. Pre-1970s, there
was no shared term for "sexual harassment." Women had the experience; the tools to
describe it as a wrong didn't exist yet. Those who hold the concept tools have the
power to make experience legible or leave it invisible.

*Why it matters for this skill*: "Sources with standing" is an application of
testimonial justice — the standing question forces the writer to ask whether they're
applying a credibility discount before evaluating what the source says. Hermeneutical
injustice explains why absence isn't neutral: if the interpretive tools belong to the
dominant group, the experience of the non-dominant group disappears from the record
even when people are trying to document it.

## Patricia Hill Collins — *Black Feminist Thought* (1990), *The Social Construction of Black Feminist Thought* (1989)

**Standpoint epistemology**: Knowledge is situated. The outsider-within position —
Black women navigating white spaces as both insiders (present, participating) and
outsiders (targets of the same structures) — generates distinctive knowledge that
insider knowledge cannot. The view from the margin sees the center and the margin;
the view from the center sees only the center.

**Matrix of domination**: Race, class, and gender don't operate as separate systems
that then interact. They're one interlocking system producing different experiences
depending on where you're located. There is no "pure" race oppression untangled from
class and gender; asking which axis is "more important" is the wrong question.

*Why it matters*: The intersectionality question in SKILL.md is downstream of this.
A Black American woman in Morocco is not a modular system — she can't be analyzed as
"Black person" + "American" + "woman." The matrix produces something that the parts
don't predict.

## Kimberlé Crenshaw — *Mapping the Margins* (1991), *Demarginalizing the Intersection* (1989)

**Intersectionality as legal concept**: Originally developed to name how anti-discrimination
law failed Black women. The employment discrimination framework asked "was this person
discriminated against as a woman?" or "as a Black person?" — but Black women faced a
form of discrimination that was *neither* alone, and fell through both frameworks.
The intersection generates a new category that the single-axis frameworks can't capture.

**Not additive**: 1 + 1 ≠ 2 here. The intersection isn't Black experience plus woman
experience. It produces experiences that neither group has alone and that you can't
derive by adding. Scholarship centered on white women's experience or Black men's
experience doesn't speak to the intersection.

*Why it matters*: The "one identity at a time" question is literally Crenshaw. The
claim that you can analyze identity along a single axis while "holding the others
constant" is the exact methodological error she named.

## Donna Haraway — *Situated Knowledges* (1988)

**The view from nowhere is a position**: Objectivity as traditionally constructed
claims a god's-eye view — seeing everything from no particular place. Haraway's
argument: that view is itself a situated position, occupied overwhelmingly by people
who look a certain way and were trained in certain traditions. The claim to be
un-positioned is a form of power, not a description of epistemic reality.

**Partial perspectives and accountability**: All knowledge is partial. The work isn't
to transcend situatedness — it's to be accountable for your position, to name it, and
to build knowledge from situated partial perspectives rather than pretending to a view
that doesn't exist.

*Why it matters*: "What can't you know, and is that stated?" is Haraway. The training
corpus has a composition. That composition is a position. Pretending it isn't is the
"view from nowhere" error. The skill doesn't ask the writer to overcome their
situatedness; it asks them to name it.

## José Medina — *The Epistemology of Resistance* (2013)

**Active ignorance**: Ignorance isn't just absence of information. It can be actively
maintained — cultivated, protected, defended against correction. Willful ignorance
about the experience of dominated groups is a form of epistemic agency, not passivity.

**Epistemic vices**: Arrogance (refusing to update on testimony from others),
epistemic laziness (not doing the work to understand what you don't know), epistemic
cowardice (avoiding the discomfort of understanding). These are virtues inverted —
their absence damages the epistemic community, not just the individual.

**Resistant knowledge**: Oppressed groups develop knowledge *about* their oppression
that the dominant group cannot develop, because you can't fully understand a system
while you're shielded from its effects. This knowledge is epistemic resource, not
grievance.

*Why it matters*: The reversal test ("if this source were *from* the community rather
than *about* it, would it carry more weight?") operationalizes the difference between
active ignorance and resistant knowledge. The weight gap IS the epistemic gap.

## bell hooks — *Talking Back* (1989), *Feminist Theory: From Margin to Center* (1984)

**Talking back as resistance**: Speaking as a subordinated subject — not talking *to*
dominant culture but *at* it, from a position of knowledge — is a political act.
The margin is not only a site of deprivation; it is a site of radical possibility, of
perspectives the center cannot generate.

**Centering the margin**: Feminist theory that centers white middle-class women's
experience is not feminist theory for everyone. The margin contains structural
knowledge about the center that the center doesn't have about itself. Starting from
the margin produces different theory than adding margin perspective onto center theory.

*Why it matters*: The inversion test ("if the assumed reader were the person this
writing is about, would the prose look different?") is hooks in practice. Writing that
treats the marginalized person as a guest in the analysis — explaining their situation
to an assumed dominant reader — cannot be rewritten into adequacy by polishing the
prose. The assumption structure is in the DNA.

## Empirical finding: RLHF makes covert bias worse

**Paper**: Hofmann et al., "AI generates covertly racist decisions about Black names
across and beyond language models" (2024, *Nature*)

**Finding**: Models trained with RLHF (Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback —
the alignment process that makes models more helpful and less overtly offensive) showed
*increased* covert discrimination against speakers of African American English. The
alignment process removed slurs and explicit bias; it deepened structural discrimination.

**Mechanism**: Human raters penalized "dialect-associated errors" as lower quality.
The feedback signal taught the model that AAE was less correct. The model learned to
prefer language patterns associated with dominant groups while appearing neutral.

*Why it matters*: The Constraint section says "this is not a confession to feel good
about — it is a working constraint." This paper is the technical substrate of that
claim. Fine-tuning for "helpfulness" doesn't flatten training corpus bias; it can
systematize and launder it. The skill is needed precisely because the alignment process
doesn't fix the problem it appears to fix.

## Gayatri Chakravorty Spivak — "Can the Subaltern Speak?" (1988)

**Vertretung vs. Darstellung**: Political representation (speaking *for* as proxy) versus re-presentation (depicting or portraying). When writing claims to "give voice" to marginalized communities, it typically conflates these: the writer substitutes their own framework while appearing merely to describe. The gap between the two is where epistemic violence happens — the community's concepts become invisible while dominant frameworks appear to illuminate them.

**Epistemic violence**: When marginalized experience is narrated through dominant-group frameworks, even sympathetically, the narration performs a harm. The community's own categories for understanding their situation are displaced by outsider categories that look like descriptions but are actually substitutions.

*Why it matters*: The "speaking for vs. depicting" question in SKILL.md is Spivak. The check is whether you're owning the substitution or pretending it isn't there.

## Ibram X. Kendi — *How to Be an Antiracist* (One World, 2019)

**The racist/antiracist binary**: Eliminates "not racist" as a stable category. Any idea either produces racial equity or sustains racial inequity — there is no neutral position. Racist and antiracist are descriptions of what someone is *doing in a given moment*, not permanent identities.

**The cause question**: The diagnostic between content that locates causes of racial disparities in the behaviors or cultures of racial groups versus content that locates them in policies and power structures. The first naturalizes inequality even when it intends to describe it neutrally.

*Why it matters*: One of the fastest diagnostics because it operates at the level of framing rather than individual claims. "Where does the causal arrow point?" cuts through a lot.

## Toni Morrison — *Playing in the Dark: Whiteness and the Literary Imagination* (Harvard UP, 1992)

**American Africanism**: The fabricated, Africanlike presence that white literary imagination constructed and used as a tool for self-definition. Defining characteristics of American literature — individualism, innocence, freedom — were built *against and dependent upon* the existence of a population that was manifestly unfree.

**The structural question**: What does the inclusion or exclusion of racial others *do for* the text? Not whether the depiction is accurate or stereotyped, but what structural function the racial other serves in the narrative — and whether that function is examined or reproduced.

*Why it matters*: Morrison gives the sharpest form to a question that's easy to miss. Even well-intentioned writing can reproduce the Africanist function. The diagnostic isn't about intention.

## Richard Dyer, Ruth Frankenberg, Sara Ahmed — on unmarked whiteness

**Richard Dyer** (*White*, Routledge, 1997): Whiteness operates as both everywhere and nowhere — creating dominant images of the world without appearing as a specific cultural position. His **commutation test**: imagine a non-white person in the role; what it reveals is how much the original depended on whiteness being unmarked.

**Ruth Frankenberg** (*White Women, Race Matters*, University of Minnesota Press, 1993): Whiteness is simultaneously a location of structural advantage, a standpoint, and a set of usually unmarked cultural practices. The invisibility of whiteness as a racial position is itself a form of power.

**Sara Ahmed** ("A Phenomenology of Whiteness," *Feminist Theory*, 2007): Declarations of anti-racism can function as **non-performative** — statements that appear to do something but produce no change, and may substitute for change. The audit, the acknowledgment, the noting of the gap — all can become ways of *not* doing the work while appearing to have done it.

*Why it matters*: The commutation test is Dyer — fast and operational. Ahmed's non-performative warning is the thing that belongs at the beginning of every checklist: naming the problem is not fixing it. This applies to this skill itself.

## Boaventura de Sousa Santos — *Epistemologies of the South* (2014)

**Abyssal thinking**: Modern Western thought draws invisible lines dividing reality into "this side" (recognized knowledge — science, law, data) and "the other side" (where entire knowledge systems are rendered literally nonexistent — not wrong, not inferior, but invisible as knowledge).

**Ecology of knowledges**: Recognize epistemological plurality. Treat different knowledge systems as incomplete but real. Ask what is being *forgotten* when something is learned.

*Why it matters*: When content positions Western science as simply "how things are" while treating other knowledge systems as ethnographic objects — even respectfully described ones — it's operating on the abyssal line. Santos makes this structural rather than a matter of individual respect or disrespect.

## Eve Tuck and K. Wayne Yang — "Decolonization Is Not a Metaphor" (2012)

**Settler moves to innocence**: Gestures that relieve guilt without relinquishing land, power, or privilege. Using "decolonize" as a synonym for diversification, inclusion, or any other reform is one of these moves — it forecloses on the specificity of Indigenous land-centered claims by making decolonization seem commensurable with everything.

*Why it matters*: Precision about language. When writing uses "decolonize" casually, it participates in the erasure it claims to oppose. The discipline of using words exactly is not pedantry; it's the minimum.

## What's missing from this tradition

Spivak, Santos, and Tuck & Yang are now in the file, which patches some of the colonial-context gap. What remains thin:

Fanon (*Black Skin, White Masks*) and Wynter (on the overrepresentation of Man as human) address the psychic and ontological dimensions of colonialism that the above tradition only partially reaches. Lugones (coloniality of gender) shows how gender as a category was itself a colonial imposition — distinct from Western feminist theory. When the writing task is outside the American racial context, these gaps still matter.

This file doesn't try to patch every gap. It names them.
