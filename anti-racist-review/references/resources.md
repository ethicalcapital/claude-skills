# Resources

Organizations, publications, and places to get corrected. Not a reading list for
its own sake — a list of things that have standing to push back.

---

## Organizations That Document What AI Gets Wrong

**Algorithmic Justice League** (algorithmic-justice-league.org)
- Documents harms from AI systems on people of color, trans people, disabled people
- Publishes case studies; accepts complaints; does public advocacy
- Has standing because it's led by and primarily serves the communities affected

**Data & Society Research Institute** (datasociety.net)
- Independent nonprofit; publishes research on AI, algorithms, and social harm
- Not led primarily by affected communities, but produces credible critical research
- Useful for documentation; not a substitute for community-led assessment

**Distributed AI Research Institute — DAIR** (dair-institute.org)
- Founded by Timnit Gebru; explicitly centers AI harms to marginalized communities
- Publishes directly; peer review not required for important findings
- Has standing on AI harm documentation; limited to AI/tech context

**AI Now Institute** (ainowinstitute.org)
- NYU-housed; publishes annual report on AI power concentration and harms
- Good on structural critique; less focused on specific incident documentation

## Community-Led Documentation (varies by context)

For any specific location or group, the relevant organizations are those:
1. Run primarily by people with the relevant identity (Afro-descendant, indigenous, etc.)
2. With documented case history — not aspirational advocacy, but actual incidents
3. That local communities trust, not government-adjacent orgs

The civil society enrichment pass (retirement-destinations research) attempted to name
these per-destination. That data is in:
`docs/reports/retirement-civil-society-poc.json`

Data confidence on that research is explicitly rated per destination. Treat low-confidence
entries as starting points, not authoritative assessments.

## Academic Work Worth Citing (by claim type)

**On AI bias in language**:
- Hofmann et al. (2024), *Nature* — RLHF and African American English discrimination
- Bender et al. (2021) — "Stochastic Parrots" — training corpus composition and harms
- Blodgett et al. (2020) — "Language (Technology) is Power" — systematic review of NLP bias literature

**On standing and epistemic injustice**:
- Fricker (2007), *Epistemic Injustice* — canonical
- Dotson (2011), "Tracking Epistemic Violence" — extends Fricker to silencing practices
- Pohlhaus (2012), "Relational Knowing and Epistemic Injustice" — on resistance and testimony

**On intersectionality (applications, not just theory)**:
- Crenshaw (1989, 1991) — original legal cases
- Carbado et al. (2013), "Intersectionality: Mapping the Movements of a Theory" — how the concept has been applied and misapplied
- Collins & Bilge (2016), *Intersectionality* — accessible entry point

**On decolonizing knowledge**:
- Tuck & Yang (2012), "Decolonization is Not a Metaphor" — on what decolonization actually requires vs. metaphorical use
- Smith (1999), *Decolonizing Methodologies* — how research on indigenous communities has worked as a tool of extraction

**On active ignorance**:
- Mills (1997), *The Racial Contract* — on white ignorance as political epistemology
- Sullivan & Tuana (2007), *Race and Epistemologies of Ignorance* — collected essays

## Where to Get Corrected

If writing reaches people with standing, the question is: can they push back?

**For Black American content about international destinations**:
- r/blacktravelmovement, r/blacktravel (Reddit) — long-tenure, Black American voices
- The Catch Me If You Can blog network (Black travel bloggers with 5+ years tenure)
- NAACP and Urban League chapters have international reach in some cities

**For content about trans people**:
- National Center for Transgender Equality (NCTE) — policy-focused, has standing
- Transgender Law Center — legal documentation of harms
- For international context: Transgender Europe (TGEU) tracks legal status country by country

**For content about disability**:
- ADAPT — direct action disability rights; run by and for disabled people
- National Federation of the Blind (NFB) — disability community-led, not charity model

**For content about immigration/undocumented**:
- United We Dream — largest undocumented-youth-led org in the US
- National Immigration Law Center (NILC) — legal documentation of harms

**General rule**: Before publishing content about a group's safety or experience,
find one organization led primarily by that group and ask: would they recognize this
picture? If you can't do that — if there's no accessible mechanism — state it
explicitly. The absence of a correction mechanism is itself information.

## Editorial Tools and Style Guides

**Conscious Style Guide** (consciousstyleguide.com, Karen Yin, 2015–present)
- Aggregates community-specific editorial guidance across race, gender, disability, and other identity categories
- Updated continuously; draws from community-specific style guides
- Practical first stop for terminology questions

**Diversity Style Guide** (Rachel Kanigel, 2016; book via Wiley, 2018)
- 700+ terms drawing from 20+ community-specific style guides including NABJ (Black journalists), NAHJ (Hispanic), AAJA (Asian American), NAJA (Native American), GLAAD, and NCDJ (disability)
- Authoritative for terminology about specific communities
- Free web version at diversitystyleguide.com

**Global Press Style Guide** (2012)
- Created with input from local women journalists in least-covered countries
- Four principles: dignity, precision, self-identification, contextual accuracy
- Represents a Global South inversion of the typical editorial apparatus — useful specifically when writing about communities in the Global South

## Diagnostic Frameworks

**Racial Equity Impact Assessment (REIA)** (Race Forward, ~2009)
- Structured questions for any proposed action or piece of content: Who is affected? Which groups could be negatively impacted? What unintended consequences might arise? How will affected communities partner in evaluation?
- Used by government agencies and nonprofits; public templates available at raceforward.org

**Maynard Institute's Fault Lines model** (Robert C. Maynard, ~1993)
- Six dimensions along which coverage typically breaks: race, gender, generation, geography, class, sexual orientation
- Pre-publication checklist: for any piece, ask whose perspective is represented and whose is missing across all six

## A Note on This List

This list has the same standing problem everything else does: it was compiled by an
AI trained on a corpus with documented composition biases. The organizations listed
are ones that made it into that corpus — which means they already have enough
institutional presence to be legible to the systems that created the record. What
isn't here may be more important than what is.

Treat this as a starting point, not an inventory.
